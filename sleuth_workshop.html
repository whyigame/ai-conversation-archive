    How the workshop works
    
    
    A Sleuth house is a 17×40 grid. Coloured cells are floor —
    everything anyone walks on. Dark cells are solid; the game
    draws the wall faces automatically wherever solid meets floor.
    There is no separate "wall" in the data: the original houses are
    rooms carved out of a solid block.
    

    
    Suggested workflow
    
    
      
      
    1. Sketch the layout with the wall brush W.
          Sketch ink is editor-only — the game never sees it — but it
          bounds the bucket, so you can rough all your rooms first.
      2. 
      
    2. Zone each room: pick a colour, turn on the bucket
          B, click inside a sketched outline to fill it.
      3. 
      
    3. Cut doors: door mode D makes the current
          room brush paint that room's door — a walkable gap. Put it in
          the solid cell between the room and a hall.
      4. 
      
    4. Specials: front door ◈ at row 15 with outdoor floor
          (16) on the bottom row beneath it. Optional staircase: floors
          12 and 13 joined by the landing pair ▼/▲ — one of each on
          the whole map. They teleport, so the two flights can live
          in different wings. You arrive on the far landing and
          step off in any open direction — just leave each landing at
          least one open side (a fully walled landing refuses entry, so
          the stairs would be useless).
      5. 
      
    5. The side columns are reserved. Column 0 gets no west
          wall (the renderer draws walls on a cell's left character,
          and column 0 has none), and column 39 sits against the black
          margin of the 1983 screen, so the workshop won't paint floors
          there at all. The top row is fair game — the border above the
          map is drawn for you — and the bottom row is where the
          outdoors goes.
      6. 
      
    6. Watch the inspector. Every stroke is re-checked
          against the engine's own rules; click a complaint to see the
          offending cells. Red slashes on the canvas mean "a player can
          never walk here".
      7. 
    
    

    
    If an area shows a small number in every cell, you painted
    doors, not floor. Doors are one-cell gaps (roomId+20); the
    overlay prints their target on each. Fix in one click: pick the
    room's floor brush, door mode off, bucket B, click the
    area.
    

    
    What the room numbers mean
    
    
      
      
    • 1–11 and 17–20 — ordinary rooms. Any room you stock
          with weapons (next milestone) becomes a guest room:
          guests sit there, and murders happen there.
      • 
      
    • 12 / 13 — the staircase (lower / upper flight). One
          guest can idle on the stair; otherwise transit.
      • 
      
    • 14 / 15 — halls. Pure transit: guests pass through but
          never linger, and nothing bad happens in a hall. 14 and 15
          behave identically — two ids exist only so one house
          can have two separate halls with their own names (House 1:
          upstairs hall 14, main hall 15). A one-hall house just uses
          15.
      • 
      
    • 16 — outdoors. The strip along the bottom; the front
          door drops you here at the start of the evening.
      • 
      
    • The secret room: exactly one weapon room may have
          no door at all. It becomes the secret room, entered
          through a hidden panel in its north wall — the engine works
          this out on its own.

    Saving
    
    
    The workshop saves nothing, anywhere, by design.
    Export JSON (it includes your sketch ink) and keep the file; import
    it to continue. When your house is furnished, the same file is what
    the game will load.
